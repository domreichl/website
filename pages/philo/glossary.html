Glossary


Acceptance.

Aligning your mind with the reality of a situation; not to be confused with goal abandonment.


Action.

An activity performed by an intentional agent; behavior at a higher level of description.


Awareness.

Seeing reality with a minimal amount of thought, emotion, ego defense, and cognitive bias.


Bad.

Harmful or obstructive; the opposite of good.


Behavior.

An activity performed by an organism.


Belief.

A verbalizable part of your model of the world.


Ego.

Hubristic pride; identifying yourself with your physical, psychological, ideological, or social state.


Ethics.

A framework for settling moral conflicts.


Evil.

Religiously upsetting.


Freedom.

The pleasure of doing what you want.


Freedom of mind.

The effectiveness of your thoughts, approximated by the ratio of real-life decisions to judgmental or ruminating thoughts.


Free will.

A remnant of outdated metaphysics, falsified both by neuroscience and self-awareness.


Goal.

A consciously pursued state of the world.


God.

Nature, linked to a mystical experience.


Good.

Useful within a given context, that is, relative to a person or group (e.g., useful to you or humanity), a goal or purpose (e.g., useful for self-preservation or well-being), and a time frame (e.g., useful from a short- or long-term perspective).


Happiness.

Medium-term pleasure, best controllable in the form of self-respect.


Individuality.

The idiosyncratic integration of socio-ecological influences on your brain throughout your life, expressed as actions for which no simple causal origin can be identified.


Intelligence.

The ability to model the world.


Joy.

Pleasure, intensified by mindfulness.


Justice.

What a people deems to be fair, best approximated by a democratic process.


Love.

Joy at someone else's happiness.


Meaning (in life).

Long-term pleasure, induced by your greater purpose, sense of belonging, responsibility, and self-transcendence, leading to a feeling of fulfillment and life satisfaction.


Meaning of life.

A category error, avoided by speaking instead of a person's meaning in life.


Mind.

A bundle of conscious states.


Mindfulness.

The mental state of being aware of the present moment without judgment.


Model.

A system of concepts and their relationships, typically expressed in symbols, propositions, often formulas, intended to explain something about the world in a necessarily simplified manner.


Morality.

A society's system of values, concretized into rights believed to facilitate cooperation.


Motive.

An unconscious state, determined by drives and emotions, that causes behavior.


Naturalism.

A model of the world according to which only natural laws and forces operate in the universe such that everything can, in theory, be explained by science; does not imply that everything is reducible to the language of scientific investigation.


Negativity.

A sign of what is wrong in your life, grounded in negative emotions.


Philosophy.

(1) A disease of reason—masqueraded as deep, critical, unconventional thinking—that obstructs common sense through misuse of language. (2) A set of general beliefs that constitute one's worldview.


Pleasure.

A positive affective state; the prime motivator of human behavior.


Positivity.

A sign of what is working well in your life, grounded in positive emotions.


Power of activity.

Willpower.


Pride.

The pleasure of knowing your power of activity; either authentic (self-respect) or hubristic (ego).


Rationality.

Valuing truth; the willingness to adjust your model of the world in the face of new evidence.


Reality.

That which is qualitatively experienced by an aware mind and quantitatively modeled by science.


Responsibility.

The ability to respond to life as it unfolds around and within you, giving meaning and substance to acts of freedom.


Rights.

Norms invented by a society to structure cooperation, expressing its values and moral sentiments.


Science.

The most reliable method for modeling the world.


Self.

A modular bundle of neuronal processes that serve multiple functions associated with the mind.


Self-awareness.

An understanding of yourself beyond the ego.


Self-discipline.

The consistent exercise of willpower, resulting in personal freedom.


Self-knowledge.

An understanding of true facts about yourself.


Self-respect.

Authentic pride; the emotional foundation of self-discipline.


Spirituality.

Valuing mindfulness; the willingness to let go of your models of the world.


Strength of will.

Stubbornness in the short term, goal fixation in the medium term, and path pursuit in the long term.


Truth.

The reliability of a model of the world.


True will.

What you want in a state of total self-awareness.


Useful.

Facilitating survival, reproduction, well-being, or goal achievement.


Value.

A belief of the form "X is good," whereby X is an abstract idea; a distilled form of wisdom.


Well-being.

A positive state of your body, mind, and life, associated with health, happiness, meaning, fulfillment, and flourishing; the highest good.


Will.

A mental state, determined by values and goals, that aims at an action.


Willpower.

The self-regulatory capacity to turn will into action.


Wisdom.

Knowing which model of the world, if any, to use in a given situation; life experience that has been reflected upon.


Zen.

The teaching that any model of the world is only a distraction from reality.
